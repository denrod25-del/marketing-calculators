Why discounts cost more than they look

A discount comes straight off your margin, not your price. If you make 40% margin and offer 10% off, your margin drops to 30% — so you now need to sell 33% more units just to earn the same gross profit. Discounts quietly demand big volume increases to pay for themselves.

The formula

Required volume increase = discount ÷ (margin − discount). The thinner your margin, the more brutal the math: at a 25% margin, a 10% discount needs 67% more volume. And if the discount ever exceeds your margin, you lose money on every single sale no matter how many you make.

Using it before you promote

Run this before launching any sale or coupon. If you don't realistically expect the volume lift it requires, the promotion destroys profit. Consider value-adds, bundles or targeted offers to fewer people instead of blanket discounts that erode margin across all buyers.

Frequently asked questions

How much more do I need to sell to cover a discount?
Divide the discount by your margin minus the discount. A 10% discount on a 40% margin needs about 33% more unit sales to break even.
What happens if the discount is bigger than my margin?
You lose money on every sale — no amount of extra volume makes it profitable.
Why do thin-margin businesses avoid discounts?
The lower the margin, the more volume a discount requires to break even, making blanket discounts especially risky.
