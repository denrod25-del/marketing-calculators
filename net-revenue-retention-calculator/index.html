What is net revenue retention?

NRR measures how much recurring revenue you keep and grow from existing customers over a period, ignoring new sales: NRR = (starting + expansion − contraction − churn) ÷ starting. Above 100% means your current customers are worth more over time even before you add anyone new — the holy grail of subscription growth.

NRR vs GRR

Gross Revenue Retention (GRR) counts only losses (contraction and churn) and caps at 100% — it shows how leaky the bucket is. NRR adds expansion, so it can exceed 100%. Strong companies pair high GRR (low churn) with expansion that pushes NRR well above 100%.

What's a good NRR?

For subscription businesses, 100%+ is healthy and 110%+ is excellent — expansion outpaces churn. Below 100% means you must keep acquiring just to stand still. Improve it by reducing churn, driving upgrades, and expanding usage within accounts.

Frequently asked questions

How do I calculate net revenue retention?
Take starting recurring revenue, add expansion, subtract contraction and churn, then divide by the starting revenue.
What is a good NRR?
100% or higher is healthy for subscription businesses; 110%+ is excellent, meaning expansion outpaces churn.
What's the difference between NRR and GRR?
GRR counts only losses and caps at 100%; NRR adds expansion revenue and can exceed 100%.
