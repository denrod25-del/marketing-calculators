What is the viral coefficient?

The viral coefficient, or K-factor, is how many new users each existing user generates through referrals: K = invites sent per user × invite conversion rate. If every user sends 5 invites and 20% convert, K = 1.0 — each user brings one more, and growth sustains itself.

What K means

A K of 1 or more means true viral growth — each cohort more than replaces itself and the loop compounds. Most real products land below 1, where virality doesn't run away on its own but still meaningfully lowers acquisition cost by supplementing paid and organic channels. Even a K of 0.5 is powerful.

Improving virality

Increase invites per user (make sharing natural and rewarding, reduce friction) or invite conversion (better landing experience, clear value, incentives on both sides). Cycle time matters too: a lower K that loops quickly can outgrow a higher K that loops slowly.

Frequently asked questions

How do I calculate the viral coefficient?
Multiply the average number of invites each user sends by the percentage of invites that convert to new users.
What is a good K-factor?
1.0 or higher means self-sustaining viral growth. Most products are below 1, where virality still lowers acquisition cost.
Why does cycle time matter?
A shorter time between invite and new signup means the loop compounds faster, so a lower K can still drive strong growth.
