What is customer lifetime value?

LTV is the total worth of a customer across the whole relationship. A simple, reliable formula is LTV = average sale × purchases per year × customer lifespan. Multiply by gross margin to get profit-based LTV, which is the number you should compare against acquisition cost.

The LTV:CAC ratio

LTV only matters relative to what it costs to acquire a customer. The LTV:CAC ratio is the headline health metric: 3:1 or higher is the widely used target. Below 1:1 you lose money on each customer; far above 5:1 you may be under-investing in growth and leaving revenue on the table.

How to raise LTV

Increase any of the three inputs: sell more per visit (upsells, bundles), get customers back more often (maintenance plans, reminders), and keep them longer (great service, loyalty). For the trades, recurring service agreements are the highest-leverage LTV move.

Frequently asked questions

How do I calculate customer lifetime value?
Multiply average sale by purchases per year and by average customer lifespan in years, then multiply by gross margin for profit-based LTV.
What is a good LTV:CAC ratio?
3:1 is the common benchmark. Below that, acquisition is too expensive; well above it, you may be able to spend more to grow faster.
Should LTV use revenue or profit?
Use gross-profit LTV (revenue × margin) when comparing to CAC — it reflects money you actually keep.
