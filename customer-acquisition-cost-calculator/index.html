What is customer acquisition cost (CAC)?

CAC is the average amount you spend to win one new customer: CAC = total sales & marketing spend ÷ new customers acquired over the same period. Include ad spend, agency and tool costs, and the salaries of the people running acquisition.

The LTV:CAC ratio

CAC only matters relative to what a customer is worth. The LTV:CAC ratio compares lifetime value to acquisition cost. A widely used benchmark is 3:1 — earn at least three dollars of lifetime value for every dollar of CAC. Below 1:1 you lose money on every customer; above 5:1 you may be under-investing in growth.

CAC payback period

Payback is how many months of gross profit per customer it takes to recover CAC: CAC ÷ (monthly revenue × gross margin). Under 12 months is generally healthy for subscription businesses.

How to lower CAC

Improve conversion rates before buying more traffic, lean into lower-cost channels (SEO, referrals, email), tighten targeting, and respond to leads faster — speed-to-lead is one of the biggest levers on close rate, which directly lowers CAC.

Frequently asked questions

What is a good CAC?
There's no universal dollar figure — it depends on customer lifetime value. Aim for an LTV:CAC ratio of at least 3:1 and a payback under 12 months.
What should I include in CAC?
All acquisition costs: ad spend, marketing tools and agencies, content, plus the salaries and commissions of sales and marketing staff.
CAC vs cost per lead?
Cost per lead is spend per lead; CAC is spend per closed customer. CAC = cost per lead ÷ lead-to-customer close rate.
