What is inventory turnover?

Inventory turnover is how many times you sell and replace your stock in a period: COGS ÷ average inventory. A turnover of 5 means you cycled through your average inventory five times in the year. Dividing 365 by turnover gives days of inventory on hand — how long stock sits before selling.

Why it matters

Inventory ties up cash and risks obsolescence. Higher turnover means you're converting stock to sales quickly and not over-investing in inventory. Too low suggests overstock, slow sellers or weak demand; extremely high can mean you're understocked and losing sales to stockouts.

Benchmarks vary

Good turnover depends heavily on the industry — grocery and fast fashion turn many times a year, while furniture or specialty goods turn far fewer. Compare to your category and your own trend, and use it alongside sell-through rate to manage buying and cash flow.

Frequently asked questions

How do I calculate inventory turnover?
Divide cost of goods sold by average inventory value. $500,000 COGS on $100,000 average inventory is 5 turns.
What are days of inventory on hand?
365 divided by your turnover ratio — the average number of days stock sits before it sells.
What is a good inventory turnover?
It's industry-specific. Grocery and fast fashion turn many times a year; big-ticket goods turn far less. Compare within your sector.
