What is the LTV:CAC ratio?

It compares what a customer is worth over their lifetime to what it cost to acquire them: LTV ÷ CAC. It's the clearest single test of whether your acquisition engine builds value or burns it — and investors and operators lean on it heavily to judge unit economics.

What's a good ratio?

The widely used benchmark is around 3:1 — roughly three dollars of lifetime value for every dollar of acquisition cost. Below 1:1 you lose money on each customer. Interestingly, a ratio that's too high (say 6:1+) can mean you're under-spending and leaving growth on the table.

Improving the ratio

Raise LTV (retention, expansion, higher prices, better margins) or lower CAC (more efficient channels, better conversion, referrals). Because LTV is the bigger lever for most businesses, retention and expansion usually move the ratio faster than cutting acquisition spend.

Frequently asked questions

How do I calculate the LTV:CAC ratio?
Divide customer lifetime value by customer acquisition cost. An LTV of $900 and CAC of $300 gives a 3:1 ratio.
What is a good LTV:CAC ratio?
Around 3:1 is the common healthy benchmark. Below 1:1 loses money; much above 5:1 may mean you're under-investing in growth.
How do I improve my LTV:CAC ratio?
Increase lifetime value through retention and expansion, or reduce acquisition cost with more efficient channels and higher conversion.
