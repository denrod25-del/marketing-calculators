Billboards & Out-of-Home Advertising

A well-placed billboard builds the kind of local familiarity that makes people call you first.

What out-of-home does well

Billboards and other out-of-home (OOH) ads build broad local awareness and familiarity. They won't drive an instant call the way search does, but seeing your name daily on the commute makes homeowners choose you first when a need arises. OOH is a brand and trust play, measured over months.

Static vs digital billboards

Static boards are cheaper and yours 24/7, ideal for a steady presence on a key route. Digital boards cost more but rotate your message, let you change creative quickly, and can run seasonal or emergency offers. For most local businesses, a well-placed static board on a high-traffic commuter route is the workhorse.

Location is everything

The value of a board is the road it sits on. Prioritize high-traffic routes through your service area, the side of the road with commuter (home-bound) traffic, and spots with a clear, uncluttered sightline. A cheaper board on the wrong road is money wasted; the right location is worth paying up for.

The seven-word rule

Drivers see a board for a few seconds. Keep it to about seven words: what you do, a big brand or phone cue, and nothing else. Huge legible type, high contrast, one simple image, and lots of empty space. If it can't be read at 60 mph, it doesn't work.

What it costs

Billboard pricing varies widely by market and traffic. A static board often runs from around a thousand dollars a month in smaller markets to several thousand or more in busy metros; digital boards are priced by share of the rotation. Ask for the location's traffic count so you can judge cost per thousand impressions.

Measuring an awareness medium

OOH is hard to attribute directly, but you can still track it: use a memorable vanity phone number or URL on the board, ask new customers how they heard about you, and watch for lifts in branded searches and direct calls after it goes up. Judge it over months, not days.

Frequently asked questions

Are billboards worth it for local businesses?
They're an awareness and familiarity play, not a direct-response channel. On the right high-traffic route through your service area, they help homeowners choose you first when a need arises.
How much does a billboard cost?
It varies by market and traffic — from around a thousand dollars a month in small markets to several thousand or more in busy metros. Always ask for the traffic count.
What should a billboard say?
About seven words at most: what you do plus a clear brand or phone cue, in huge legible type with high contrast and plenty of empty space.
