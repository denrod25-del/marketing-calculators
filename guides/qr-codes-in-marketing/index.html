Using QR Codes in Marketing

QR codes turn a truck, flyer or yard sign into a one-scan path to book you.

Why QR codes came back

Every modern phone camera scans QR codes natively now, and people are used to it. That revived the humble QR code as a bridge between the physical world and your website — one scan takes someone from your truck, flyer or sign straight to booking, no typing a URL required.

Bridge offline to online

The magic of a QR code is closing the gap between seeing your marketing and acting on it. A homeowner who spots your yard sign can scan and book in seconds instead of remembering your name and searching later. Removing that friction captures leads you'd otherwise lose.

Where to place them

Put QR codes where people have a moment to scan: vehicle wraps and yard signs, flyers and door hangers, invoices and business cards, print ads and event booths. Add a short instruction and reason ("Scan for a free estimate") so people know why to bother.

What to link to

Send scans to a focused action, not your homepage — a booking page, a special offer, a review page, or a specific service. Match the destination to where the code lives: an invoice code might request a review, while a yard-sign code should book an estimate.

Track every scan

Use a dynamic QR code or a tracked URL so you can see how many scans each placement drives. This turns previously unmeasurable offline marketing — signs, wraps, flyers — into something you can actually attribute and optimize.

Design that gets scanned

Keep codes large enough to scan from a distance (especially on vehicles and signs), high-contrast, and uncluttered. Always pair them with a clear call to action and, ideally, the offer. A code with no reason to scan gets ignored.
