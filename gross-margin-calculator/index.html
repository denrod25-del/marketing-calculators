Margin vs markup — the key difference

They're often confused. Gross margin = profit ÷ price (profit as a share of what you charge). Markup = profit ÷ cost (profit as a share of what it cost you). A 50% markup on a $60 item is a $30 profit and a $90 price — but only a 33% margin. Same dollars, very different percentages.

Pricing from a target margin

To hit a target margin, price is cost ÷ (1 − margin). For a 50% margin on a $60 cost, you must charge $120 — not $90. Marking up by your target-margin percentage under-prices you, which is a common and costly mistake.

Why margin matters for marketing

Margin sets how much you can afford to spend to acquire a customer and your break-even ROAS (1 ÷ margin). Higher-margin products and services can outbid competitors on ads and still profit. Know your margin before you set budgets.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between margin and markup?
Margin is profit as a percent of price; markup is profit as a percent of cost. The same dollar profit gives a lower margin than markup.
How do I calculate selling price from a target margin?
Divide cost by (1 minus the target margin as a decimal). For a 50% margin on $60 cost, charge $120.
Why does margin matter for marketing?
It determines how much you can spend to acquire a customer and your break-even ROAS, which is 1 divided by your margin.
