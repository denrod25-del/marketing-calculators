Does a discount actually make money?

A discount cuts the margin on every sale — including the customers who would have paid full price. To come out ahead, the extra volume has to more than cover that lost margin. This tool compares profit with the promotion to profit without it, so you see the true incremental result before you run it.

The break-even lift

Every promotion has a break-even extra units figure — the additional sales you must generate just to offset the discount on your baseline volume. If you don't realistically expect that lift, the promo destroys profit even if total sales look higher. Deep discounts on thin margins need enormous volume to work.

Smarter promotions

Protect margin: prefer added value (bundles, free add-ons) over straight price cuts, target discounts at new or lapsed customers rather than loyal full-price buyers, and set an end date. Always model the incremental profit — "more sales" and "more profit" are not the same thing.

Frequently asked questions

How do I know if a discount is profitable?
Compare profit with the promo (all units at the discounted margin) to profit without it (baseline units at full margin). If incremental profit is positive, it pays.
What is break-even lift?
The extra units you must sell just to offset the margin lost by discounting your baseline volume. Below it, the promo loses money.
Are discounts bad?
Not inherently, but they cut margin on every sale. Added-value offers and targeting lapsed customers often beat straight price cuts.
